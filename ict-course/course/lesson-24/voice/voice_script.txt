Welcome back. Today we're covering the single most important topic in forex trading — Risk Management.

You can have the best entry strategy in the world, but without proper risk management, your account will eventually blow up. Trading forex is as much about protecting your capital as it is about making profits. A disciplined risk management plan ensures longevity in the markets and prevents emotional decision-making from taking over.

Let's get into the rules.

This is the foundation. Never risk more than 2% of your total capital on a single trade. Period.

Here's why. If you risk 10% per trade and take three losses in a row, you're down 30%. That's a deep hole to climb out of. But if you risk 2% per trade, three losses only cost you 6%. Your account stays healthy, and you can keep trading.

Let's do the math. Suppose your capital is $10,000. Two percent of that is $200. So on any single trade, your maximum loss is $200. You calculate position size based on where your stop loss sits so that if price hits your stop, you lose exactly $200 — no more, no less.

This rule keeps you alive through the inevitable losing streaks.

Here's a smart adjustment. If you hit a 2% loss in a single trading session, reduce your risk per trade to 1% until you regain profitability and stabilize your account balance.

Why? Because after a loss, your emotions are elevated. You're more likely to revenge trade or take reckless setups. Cutting your risk in half forces you to slow down and protects you from yourself.

Once you're back in profit and your balance is stable, you can return to the normal 2% rule.

Every trade setup should have a minimum risk-to-reward ratio of 1:3. For every dollar you risk, you aim to make three.

This is mathematically powerful. With a 1:3 R:R, you only need to win 25% of your trades to break even. Win 30% or 40%, and you're profitable. You don't need to be right most of the time — you just need your winners to be bigger than your losers.

Example: if your stop loss is 20 pips, your take profit should be at least 60 pips. Never take a trade where your potential profit is smaller than your risk.

Overtrading is one of the fastest ways to lose money. Limit yourself to one or two high-quality setups per day.

Look for confluences: support and resistance levels, trend confirmation, and strong price action signals. If a setup doesn't check multiple boxes, skip it. The market will always give you another opportunity.

Remember, you don't have to trade every day. Sometimes the best trade is no trade at all.

Always set your stop loss and take profit before you enter a trade. This is non-negotiable.

And here's the key — never adjust your stop loss to make it wider if the trade goes against you. That's called moving the goalposts. You're essentially increasing your risk after the fact, which defeats the entire purpose of your risk management plan.

Stick to the plan. Accept losses as part of the business. It's better to take a small, planned loss than to hold on and hope, turning a small loss into a big one.

Set a daily loss limit and honor it. A reasonable number is 4% of your total capital.

If you hit that limit, stop trading for the day. Close your charts, walk away, and come back tomorrow.

This prevents revenge trading — the urge to immediately try to win back what you lost. Revenge trading almost always leads to more losses and emotional burnout. A fresh mindset tomorrow is worth more than a desperate trade tonight.

Don't put all your risk on a single currency pair or one type of setup. Diversify across different forex pairs and market conditions.

Different pairs move at different times. Spreading your risk reduces the impact of a single bad trade or a sudden market move on one pair.

This ties everything together. You can have perfect rules, but if you can't follow them, they're worthless.

Losses are inevitable. Every trader loses — the difference between successful traders and blown accounts is how they manage those losses. Stick to your plan, keep a trading journal, and review your mistakes. Analyze what went wrong and refine your approach over time.

Following this risk management plan ensures steady growth and protects your capital. Trading is a marathon, not a sprint. Focus on discipline, patience, and consistent execution. Do that, and you give yourself the best possible chance at long-term success.
