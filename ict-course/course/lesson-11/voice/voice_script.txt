Liquidity zones are areas on a price chart where most stop orders are resting. Smart money—the institutions and market makers—actively try to sweep these zones to trigger those stops.

Identifying these liquidity zones helps you anticipate potential price moves. When you can see where price is being drawn toward, you can position yourself ahead of the move instead of chasing it.

In this lesson we will cover the first and most foundational type: **Buy Side Liquidity** and **Sell Side Liquidity**.

---

**On screen:** Definition text: "Liquidity = availability of willing buyers and sellers at market price"

The word liquidity itself means convertible to cash. An asset has high liquidity if it can easily be sold and converted to cash. But in trading, liquidity means the availability of willing buyers and sellers at a given market price.

Markets need liquidity to function. When there are many buyers and sellers, orders fill quickly with minimal slippage. When liquidity is thin, price can gap and move erratically.

ICT teaches that price moves between liquidity pools. Price does not move randomly—it is delivered from one liquidity zone to another. Understanding where liquidity sits is the key to understanding where price is likely to go.

---

**On screen:** Chart diagram showing a high with stacked buy stop orders above it, labeled "Buy Stops — Buy Side Liquidity"

Buy side liquidity, according to ICT, is the volume of pending buy orders—specifically buy stop orders.

When traders execute a sell order, most of them want to protect that position in case price moves against them. So they place a buy stop order above their entry as a hedge. If price rises and hits that buy stop, it closes their losing sell position.

Because of this, anyone selling at a price level will have buy stop orders placed above that price. These buy stops accumulate above swing highs.

That is why old highs—like the week's high, the day's high, or equal highs—are termed **buy side liquidity**. Market makers know these buy stops are sitting there. So they push price up to grab those highs, triggering the buy stop orders and converting them into market orders.

**On screen:** "Buy stops above highs = Buy Side Liquidity"

Once those orders are filled, the market makers have the liquidity they need to move price in the opposite direction, trapping late buyers who chased the breakout. This is the classic **liquidity hunt**.

---

**On screen:** Chart diagram showing a low with stacked sell stop orders below it, labeled "Sell Stops — Sell Side Liquidity"

Now let's look at the other side. Sell side liquidity refers to the accumulation of pending sell orders, particularly sell stop orders.

When traders initiate buy orders, they often place sell stop orders below their entry to safeguard their position. If price drops and hits that sell stop, the position is closed to prevent further loss.

These sell stops accumulate below significant lows. So old lows—weekly lows, daily lows, or equal lows—are loaded with sell side liquidity.

**On screen:** "Sell stops below lows = Sell Side Liquidity"

Market makers target these established lows. They push price down through them, triggering the sell stop orders and converting them into market orders. Once the sell stops are filled, smart money has the liquidity to reverse price back up, trapping breakout sellers who expected further downside.

---

**On screen:** Side-by-side diagram showing buy side sweep (price spikes above high, reverses down) and sell side sweep (price spikes below low, reverses up)

Both buy side and sell side liquidity follow the same pattern. Market makers need volume to enter or exit large positions. Retail stop orders provide that volume.

The mechanism works like this:
1. Retail traders place stops above highs (sell protection) or below lows (buy protection).
2. Smart money drives price into those zones, triggering the stops.
3. The triggered stops become market orders, giving smart money the liquidity to execute their own trades.
4. Smart money then reverses price, leaving retail traders on the wrong side of the move.

This is called a **liquidity hunt** or **stop hunt**. It happens every single day across all timeframes.

---

**On screen:** Chart example showing a daily timeframe with a clear old high, price breaking above it then reversing sharply

Let's walk through a real scenario. Look at this daily chart. You see an old high formed two weeks ago. Price comes back up to that level and breaks above it by a few pips, taking out the buy stops.

Most retail traders see this breakout and think the market is going higher. They pile into buy orders. But instead of continuing up, price immediately reverses and drops hundreds of pips.

What happened? Smart money swept the buy side liquidity above the old high, filled their sell orders into that liquidity, and then drove price down. The breakout traders are now trapped in losing longs.

The same works in reverse for sell side liquidity. Price breaks below an old low, sell stops are triggered, retail shorts pile in, and smart money reverses price upward.
