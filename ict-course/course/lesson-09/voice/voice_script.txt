Sometimes you see price open and move rapidly, leaving behind a **zone** where trading activity looks “thin.”

ICT calls that zone a **vacuum block**.

In other words: price left a temporary **vacuum of liquidity**, and the market often revisits it.

---

From the book:

- “ICT vacuum block is a gap created in price action because of a high volatility event…”
- It can also be created by opening gaps (week/day/session opening).
- “It is called vacuum block because of the vacuum of liquidity.”

Core idea:
- There has been **no trading activity** in the gap (no meaningful fills)
- So price often later **fills the gap** and then **continues** the move.

---

The book lists examples:
- high volatility events like **FOMC, NFP**
- geopolitical events like **war**

Teach this clearly:
- Big news → sudden repricing → liquidity can’t “trade smoothly”
- That creates a gap/inefficiency
- Later, price tends to return to rebalance and fill

---

**Bullish vacuum block** logic from the book:

- “Bullish vacuum block is created when price opens above the current market price leaving a gap behind it…”

Interpretation:
- The market opens up sharply.
- Because of volatility, there’s a vacuum of liquidity in the gap.
- Price often **fills** the vacuum first.
- After filling, it can **reprices** and continue in the uptrend direction.

---

**Bearish vacuum block** logic from the book (mirror concept):

- A bearish vacuum indicates the market opens down sharply, leaving a gap.
- Liquidity vacuum forms.
- Price tends to fill the gap.
- Then it reprices and continues (typically in the bearish direction context).

> Key teaching emphasis:  
> Vacuum blocks are about **gap fill + continuation**, driven by liquidity vacuum.

---

In a real trading system, don’t treat vacuum blocks as standalone triggers.

Use them like:
- a **zone of likely revisiting**
- which you confirm with:
  - liquidity events,
  - PD-Array premium/discount context,
  - and market structure shift.
