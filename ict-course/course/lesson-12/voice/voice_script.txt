Some liquidity levels are easy for price to sweep. Others take a long time—price struggles, grinds, and tests the same level multiple times before finally breaking through.

ICT names these **high resistance liquidity** and **low resistance liquidity**. And on top of that, ICT also organizes liquidity by whether it sits **inside** or **outside** a defined range.

Let's break down both concepts.

---

From the book:

When you look at an old high on the chart, and that old high has **many swing highs defending it**, ICT calls that area **high resistance liquidity**.

The same goes for an old low: if many swing lows are stacked defending it, that's also high resistance liquidity.

**Why it matters:**
- Price doesn't slice through high resistance liquidity in one quick move.
- Instead, price may form multiple touches, fakeouts, and consolidations before finally sweeping it.

**High Resistance Liquidity Run (HRLR):**
- This is the scenario where price **struggles** to sweep out liquidity.
- It takes a **long time** to reach the old swing high or swing low.
- The level is defended by multiple short-term highs and lows stacked in the way.

Think of HRLR like trying to push through a door with multiple locks—each swing high or swing low is another lock that needs to be broken before the old high or low gets taken.

---

In contrast, the **short-term swing highs and lows** that form *between* an old high and an old low are called **low resistance liquidity**.

These are easy for price to sweep out because they don't have the same defensive structure behind them.

**Low Resistance Liquidity Run (LRLR):**
- Price moves with **aggressive acceleration**.
- It cuts through these short-term levels quickly.
- The fast move often leaves behind **liquidity voids** or **fair value gaps (FVGs)**.

From the book (Page 67):

> "In this scenario, the price moves quickly and easily through areas of liquidity due to the presence of fewer resistance points."

**Key distinction:**
- HRLR = slow, grinding, multiple tests, price takes time
- LRLR = fast, impulsive, leaves FVGs, price accelerates

When you see a strong impulsive move with a visible FVG left behind, that's a signature of low resistance liquidity being swept.

---

Now we shift to **internal and external range liquidity**, and this requires understanding the **ICT dealing range**.

From the book:

The ICT dealing range is the area between:
- An **established Swing High** that has already taken the liquidity of an old high
- An **established Swing Low** that has already taken the liquidity of an old low

In other words:
1. Price sweeps an old high → that becomes your swing high reference
2. Price sweeps an old low → that becomes your swing low reference
3. The space between them = the **dealing range**

Everything inside that range has a specific name, and everything outside it has another.

---

Anything that sits **between** the swing high and swing low of the dealing range is termed **internal range**.

But here's the specific rule from the book:

> "Only the Fair Value Gap is marked as the ICT internal range liquidity."

So not every candle or structure inside the dealing range counts. Only the **Fair Value Gap** is treated as internal range liquidity.

**Why this matters:**
- When price is inside the dealing range, you're watching for FVGs to act as support or resistance within that range.
- These FVGs are the internal liquidity targets that price may fill before deciding whether to break out of the range.

---

The boundaries of the dealing range—the swing high and swing low themselves—are termed **external range**.

From the book:

- The **high** of the ICT dealing range = **buy-side liquidity** (assuming buy stops rest above it)
- The **low** of the ICT dealing range = **sell-side liquidity** (assuming sell stops rest below it)

**Conceptual model:**
- External range = the "walls" of the dealing range
- Internal range = FVGs inside the "room" between the walls

**Trading implication:**
- When price is inside the dealing range, it may first fill internal FVGs.
- Then it will eventually target one of the external bounds—either sweeping the high (buy-side) or the low (sell-side).
- The direction of the breakout tells you which side of liquidity was the intended target.

---

Here's how these two frameworks connect:

1. **High resistance liquidity** = old highs/lows defended by many swing points. Price takes time to sweep them (HRLR).
2. **Low resistance liquidity** = short-term swing points between extremes. Price sweeps them fast and leaves FVGs (LRLR).
3. **ICT dealing range** = the range between a swing high that swept old high liquidity and a swing low that swept old low liquidity.
4. **Internal range liquidity** = FVGs inside the dealing range.
5. **External range liquidity** = the swing high (buy-side) and swing low (sell-side) of the dealing range.

**Example workflow:**
1. Identify a swing high that has taken out an old high and a swing low that has taken out an old low.
2. Mark the dealing range between them.
3. Inside the range, look for FVGs as internal liquidity.
4. Outside the range, the high and low are external buy-side and sell-side liquidity.
5. Watch whether price fills internal FVGs first, then which external bound it breaks—that tells you the directional bias.
