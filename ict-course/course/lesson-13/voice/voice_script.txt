Most retail traders buy or sell at market price. Smart money buys below market and sells above it.

So the question becomes: **where are the retail traders most likely to put their orders?**

That zone — the cluster of retail orders — is your **liquidity pool**. And once you know where the pool sits, you can anticipate how price will interact with it.

---

From the book (page 72):

Retail traders tend to:
- **Buy above an old high after a breakout** — expecting continuation
- **Sell below an old low after a breakout** — expecting continuation
- Place **sell stops below old lows** to protect longs
- Place **buy stops above old highs** to protect shorts

That means:
- **Old highs** have a pool of liquidity **resting above them** (buy stops + breakout buyers)
- **Old lows** have a pool of liquidity **resting below them** (sell stops + breakout sellers)

A liquidity pool is simply a price level where a large number of orders are waiting. Smart money targets these pools to fill their own large positions.

**Chart example to show:**
Draw an old high, a zone above it labeled "Buy Stops + Buyers." Draw an old low, a zone below it labeled "Sell Stops + Sellers." That is your liquidity pool.

---

From the book (page 73):

"Liquidity" means willing buyers or sellers. "Void" means absence. Put them together: **ICT liquidity void** is the absence of buyers or sellers in a particular zone.

**When does it happen?**
- Price breaks out of a consolidation phase
- Moves strongly in one direction
- Does **not** retrace
- Leaves candlesticks with **no wicks** inside prior candle ranges

That imbalance — those gap-like candles — is the liquidity void.

**What it tells you:**
There was no meaningful trading in that zone. Price ran through it so fast that orders couldn't fill. The market may later return to **rebalance** that void — similar to how a fair value gap behaves.

**Chart example:**
Show a tight consolidation, then a strong impulsive move with gap-like candles. Highlight the empty space between the consolidation and the current price. Label it "Liquidity Void."

---

From the book (pages 74–76):

A **liquidity sweep** is a price movement designed to **capture liquidity** and then **reverse direction**.

This is the classic "stop hunt":
- Price pushes past an old high or old low
- Triggers all the stop-losses and breakout orders sitting there
- Then immediately reverses

**Key trait:** the reversal happens right after the sweep. It is a **fakeout**.

**Sequence to narrate:**
1. Price approaches a level with a known liquidity pool (e.g., above an old high)
2. Price briefly pushes past it — triggering buy stops and attracting breakout buyers
3. Buyers are now trapped at the high
4. Price reverses sharply and heads the other direction

That is a sweep. It grabbed the liquidity and turned.

---

From the book (pages 74–76):

A **liquidity run** also captures liquidity — but unlike a sweep, price **continues in the same direction**.

**How it happens:**
- Price moves toward a liquidity pool (e.g., above an old high)
- Breaks past it, triggering stops and attracting breakout orders
- Those very orders add fuel to the move
- Price keeps running in the same direction

**Key difference:**
| Concept | Behavior |
|---------|----------|
| Sweep | Captures liquidity → **reverses** |
| Run | Captures liquidity → **continues** |

This distinction matters for your trade plan. A sweep signals a reversal setup. A run signals trend continuation.

---

Walk through a real-looking chart example:

1. Identify old highs/old lows → mark liquidity pools above and below
2. Watch price approach a pool
3. If price pushes through and immediately reverses = **sweep** → look for entries in the opposite direction
4. If price pushes through and keeps going = **run** → look for continuation entries

Context matters:
- Run = grab the pool, use it as fuel to reach the next pool
- Sweep = grab the pool, trap traders, then reverse to grab the opposite pool

Both are smart money tactics. Your job is to identify which one is playing out in real time.
