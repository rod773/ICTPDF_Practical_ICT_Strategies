Markets don’t move perfectly smoothly.  
Sometimes price moves in a way that leaves an area where price didn’t “trade enough.”

ICT describes that inefficiency using a structure called the **Fair Value Gap**.

---

From the book:

- ICT fair value gap is a **three-candle structure**
- It indicates a **gap between the high and low of the 1st and 3rd candlestick**
- The “gap” exists because price moved quickly and **did not retrace** inside that zone

**Simple mental model:**  
- Candle 1 sets a boundary
- Candle 2 creates imbalance with strong body range
- Candle 3 extends the move, leaving an inefficiency between candle 1 and 3

---

The book’s identification steps:

1) Look for a **large candlestick** with the **most body range**  
   (this is usually the “impulsive” middle candle)

2) Mark:
   - the **high of the candlestick prior** to the large candle
   - the **low of the subsequent candlestick** after the large candle

3) You should be able to see a **visible gap** between those two levels  
   That gap = the **ICT Fair Value Gap**

---

From the book:

A **bullish FVG** appears during an uptrend with a three-candle pattern.

It happens when:
- the **middle candle** has a large body
- the movement creates a gap between:
  - the **high of the first candle**
  - and the **low of the third candle**

**How it behaves:**  
In an uptrend, the FVG can act as **strong support**, and often price retraces to **fill the gap** before continuing higher.

---

From the book:

A **bearish FVG** appears in a downtrend within a three-candle pattern.

It forms when:
- the **middle candle** has a large body
- the gap is between:
  - the **low of the first candle**
  - and the **high of the third candle**

**How it behaves:**  
In bearish trend, the FVG can act as **resistance**, and price often fills it before moving lower.
