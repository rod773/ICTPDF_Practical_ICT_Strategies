Market structure is the foundation of ICT trading. It tells you the **framework** within which a market is trading at any given time — and it provides insight into the market's behavior, condition, and current flow.

Without understanding market structure, you're just guessing. With it, you can see whether the market is trending up, trending down, or ranging — and more importantly, when that trend is about to change.

In this lesson we go beyond simple swing highs and swing lows. We're going to classify them into **Short Term**, **Intermediate Term**, and **Long Term** — for both highs and lows. This is the hierarchy the smart money uses.

---

Before we dive into the hierarchy, let's establish the three basic types:

**Bullish Market Structure:** Consistently rising prices. Price makes higher long-term highs and higher long-term lows. Each pullback finds support at a level higher than the last one.

**Bearish Market Structure:** Consistently falling prices. Price makes lower long-term lows and lower long-term highs. Each rally finds resistance at a level lower than the last one.

**Sideways Market Structure:** Price trades in a range, making equal highs and equal lows. No clear direction. Also called consolidation or accumulation.

Understanding which of these three you're in determines your entire trading bias.

---

An ICT Short Term High is a **swing high** formed by a three-candle formation.

The rule: the high (the wick) of the middle candle must be **higher** than the high of both the candle on its left and the candle on its right.

That's it. Three candles. Middle candle's high is above both neighbors. That middle candle's high is your STH.

**On screen:** Diagram showing three candles — middle candle's wick clearly higher than left and right candles.

These are the smallest meaningful highs in the ICT framework. They form constantly on lower timeframes and are the building blocks for the larger structures.

---

An Intermediate Term High is a step up in the hierarchy.

An ITH is basically a Short Term High — but with a lower Short Term High on its **left** and a lower Short Term High on its **right**.

Think of it as an STH that is framed by two lower STHs. The middle STH becomes the ITH.

**On screen:** Diagram showing three STHs — the middle one is higher than the one on the left and the one on the right. Label the middle one "ITH".

This creates a fractal structure. STHs nest inside ITHs. You can't have an ITH without first having STHs to compare.

---

Now we go to the top of the hierarchy.

A Long Term High is also an Intermediate Term High by nature — but with a key difference: it is **mostly formed at a higher timeframe PD Array** after a reaction of price.

An LTH is the **highest** Intermediate Term High, with a **lower** Intermediate Term High on its left and a **lower** Intermediate Term High on its right.

**On screen:** Diagram showing three ITHs — the middle one is the highest. Label it "LTH". Show a higher timeframe PD Array zone behind it.

In a bullish market structure, price makes **higher LTHs** and **higher LTLs**. If price is supposed to continue higher, it should **not** violate the recent higher long-term low.

This is your major structural level. When price breaks below a recent higher LTL, the bullish structure is in danger.

---

Now let's flip to the downside.

An ICT Short Term Low is a **swing low** formed by a three-candle formation.

The rule: the low (the wick) of the middle candle must be **lower** than the low of both the candle on its left and the candle on its right.

**On screen:** Diagram showing three candles — middle candle's wick clearly lower than left and right candles.

Just like STHs, STLs are the smallest meaningful lows. They form constantly and are the building blocks for ITLs and LTLs.

---

An Intermediate Term Low is a Short Term Low with a **higher** Short Term Low on its left and a **higher** Short Term Low on its right.

Notice the pattern: for ITHs we looked for lower STHs on either side. For ITLs we look for **higher** STLs on either side. The middle STL, being the lowest of the three, becomes your ITL.

**On screen:** Diagram showing three STLs — the middle one is lower than the left and right STLs. Label it "ITL".

ITLs are the intermediate support levels. They matter more than STLs but less than LTLs.

---

A Long Term Low is the most significant low structure.

An LTL is also an Intermediate Term Low by nature — but it is **mostly formed at a higher timeframe PD Array**.

An LTL is the **lowest** Intermediate Term Low, with a **higher** Intermediate Term Low on its left and a **higher** Intermediate Term Low on its right.

**On screen:** Diagram showing three ITLs — the middle one is the lowest. Label it "LTL". Show a higher timeframe PD Array zone behind it.

In a bearish market structure, price makes **lower LTLs** and **lower LTHs**. If price breaks above the recent lower LTH, the bearish structure is no longer valid.

---

Here's the practical application:

**Bullish structure check:**
- Price makes higher LTHs and higher LTLs
- If price violates the most recent higher LTL, it is **no longer bullish**
- That violation is your signal that a reversal or deep retracement is coming

**Bearish structure check:**
- Price makes lower LTLs and lower LTHs
- If price breaks **above** the most recent lower LTH, it is **no longer bearish**
- That break tells you the downtrend is failing

**The hierarchy matters:**
- Don't get distracted by STH/STL breaks — those are normal
- ITH/ITL breaks are more significant
- LTH/LTL breaks signal a structural shift

This is how you move from looking at random swings to reading the market's actual delivery system. You stop trading every wiggle and start trading the structure that matters.
