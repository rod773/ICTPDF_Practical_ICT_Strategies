Price does not move randomly. It oscillates between two types of liquidity — external range liquidity and internal range liquidity. This cycle repeats constantly, bar after bar, day after day. When you understand it, you stop reacting to price moves and start anticipating them.

Let me show you how this works from the book.

Page 120 of the ICT trading book states it clearly: price moves from "external to internal" and then "internal to external" liquidity. This cycles over and over.

What does that mean in plain terms? Think of the dealing range we covered in earlier lessons. The swing high and swing low of that range are the external boundaries. Those are your external range liquidity. Inside the dealing range sits the Fair Value Gap — that is your internal range liquidity.

Here is how the cycle plays out in a bullish trend. Price rises to take the external range liquidity, which is the swing high of the dealing range. That sweep grabs the buy-side liquidity resting above that old high. But price cannot just keep going up forever. After taking that external liquidity, price drops back down to balance the move. It comes inside the range and takes the internal range liquidity — the FVG sitting inside the dealing range. It fills that gap, rebalances, and then prepares for the next leg up.

Now flip it for a bearish trend. Price drops down first to take the external range liquidity on the sell side — the swing low of the dealing range. After sweeping that low, price rallies back up into the range to fill the internal FVG. Then it continues lower again.

The key takeaway: price is always alternating. External sweep, internal fill, external sweep, internal fill. When you see one, start looking for the other.

Now let us layer in the high resistance and low resistance liquidity concepts from earlier lessons. Pages 122 to 123 of the book show how this connects.

In a bullish trend, price will move up to take a high resistance liquidity run. That HRLR is an old high with many swing highs stacked in front of it defending it. Price grinds through those defenses slowly. It tests, it consolidates, it fakes out. Eventually it sweeps that old high and grabs the liquidity above it.

Once that HRLR is taken, what happens next? Price comes back down to take the low resistance liquidity run. Those LRLR targets are the short-term swing lows that were created during the previous bullish move. Unlike the grind up to the HRLR, this move down is fast. It accelerates. It leaves FVGs behind. There is no resistance defending those short-term lows, so price cuts right through them.

Now the reverse in a bearish trend. Price drops first to take the HRLR on the sell side — an old low defended by many swing lows. That grind down takes time and multiple touches. After sweeping that external HRLR, price rallies back up quickly to take the LRLR targets — the short-term swing highs from the previous bearish leg. That rally is fast and leaves FVGs.

The book breaks this down into a repeatable three-step sequence.

Step one: price targets external range liquidity. That is the swing high in a bullish trend or the swing low in a bearish trend. This is the HRLR side of the move. It takes time.

Step two: after sweeping that external liquidity, price returns to balance the internal range liquidity — the FVG inside the dealing range. This is the rebalancing phase.

Step three: once the internal FVG is filled, price targets the next external range liquidity. The cycle starts over.

This creates a constant oscillation. Internal to external, external to internal. Understanding this rhythm lets you anticipate where price is likely to go next. If you just saw a sweep of the dealing range high, you should be looking for a drop back into the range to fill an FVG. If you just saw an FVG get filled inside the range, you should be looking for a breakout toward the opposite external boundary.

Grab a daily or 4-hour chart. Identify a dealing range — a swing high that has taken an old high and a swing low that has taken an old low. Mark those two external levels. Inside the range, find the most prominent FVG. That is your internal liquidity. Now watch. When price breaks above the swing high, it is taking external range liquidity. Wait for it to return to the FVG. That is your rebalance. After the FVG fills, look for the next external target.

That is the cycle. Watch it play out live and you will see it repeat hundreds of times across every timeframe.
