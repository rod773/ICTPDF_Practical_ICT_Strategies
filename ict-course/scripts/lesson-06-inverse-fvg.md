## Lesson Script

### 1) Hook: when a “good” FVG stops working (0:00–1:30)
In ICT, a Fair Value Gap (FVG) is an inefficiency.  
Often, after it forms, price behaves as if that zone is important—like support or resistance.

But what if price **violates** the FVG instead of respecting it?

That scenario leads to the next structure: the **Inverse Fair Value Gap**.

---

### 2) Definition: what is an inverse FVG? (1:30–4:30)
From the book:

- An **inverse fair value gap** forms when a fair value gap **fails to hold**
- Price goes **beyond** and breaks the fair value gap
- Inverse FVG signals the **most initial shift in momentum**
- That shift can result in:
  - a short retracement, or
  - a change in direction later

**Key idea to repeat on camera:**  
“Inverse FVG = FVG fails → price breaks through → momentum shifts early.”

---

### 3) The “failure” condition (4:30–7:30)
To identify an inverse FVG, you need to see this sequence:

1) A fair value gap exists (from earlier price action)
2) Price attempts to respect it
3) Instead of holding, price **breaks through the FVG**
4) That break is what you interpret as the **inverse FVG signal**

So you’re no longer treating the FVG as a support/resistance zone—you’re treating it as proof that the market delivery logic changed.

---

### 4) Trading interpretation (7:30–11:30)
The inverse FVG is valuable because it helps you detect:
- early momentum changes,
- potential short retracements, or
- a later directional shift.

**How to teach it clearly:**
- “When the FVG fails, the market shows it is no longer respecting inefficiency as expected.”
- “The market is repricing. That repricing often begins with a momentum shift.”

---
