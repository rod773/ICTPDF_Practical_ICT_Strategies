## Lesson Script

### 1. What is the ICT Asian Range?

The ICT Asian Range is simply the high-to-low range of the Asian session. In New York Local Time, this session runs from **07:00 PM to 12:00 AM midnight**.

Why does this matter? Because the algorithm often uses the Asian Range as a reference point for the London and New York sessions. If price stays inside a tight consolidation during this window, it's not random — it's compression. And compression leads to expansion.

A tight Asian Range often signals that a trending algorithm is about to take over. The market will likely sweep out liquidity — either above the high or below the low of that range — trapping retail traders, then reversing.

Think of it like a spring being coiled. The tighter the coil, the stronger the snap.

### 2. How to Trade the Asian Range

You must have a **directional bias** first. Don't skip this step.

1. Mark the high and low of the Asian session (07:00 PM — 12:00 AM NY time).
2. Wait for price to break and sweep beyond either the high or the low.
3. If the Asian low is taken, look for a **CISD** (Change in State of Delivery) or **MSS** (Market Structure Shift) on a lower timeframe — then go long.
4. If the Asian high is taken, reverse the logic — wait for a CISD or MSS and go short.

The sweep traps the herd. You enter when the algorithm confirms the trap with a shift in structure.

### 3. New York Session Trading Strategy

The New York session opens at **08:00 AM NY local time**. Here's a reliable setup:

If price already took London liquidity and moved away, the NY session often sees a retracement before continuation.

1. Draw a **Fibonacci retracement** from the London session low to the high (prior to the NY retracement).
2. Wait for price to tap an **ICT OTE level** (Optimal Trade Entry — typically the 0.62 or 0.79 retracement).
3. Execute only after a **MSS on the 1-minute** timeframe.

This gives you a high-probability entry in the direction of the London move, with defined risk and a reason for the retracement.

### 4. What are ICT Macros?

ICT defines a macro as:

> "A short order of instructions that creates an event in price delivery."

In plain English: Macros are specific time windows when the algorithm is programmed to act. During these windows, the algorithm either hunts liquidity or reprices a fair value gap.

Key points:
- An ICT macro happens **every single hour**.
- It spans the **last 10 minutes of the closing hour** and the **first 10 minutes of the opening hour**.
- That's a 20-minute window every hour where you should pay close attention.

Additionally, the **last hour of a session** contains four macros, one every 15 minutes.

### 5. How ICT Macros Work in Practice

During macro times, expect one of two things:

- **Liquidity hunt** — the algorithm sweeps sell-side or buy-side liquidity.
- **FVG rebalancing** — price moves to fill or rep a fair value gap.

Your trading setup may form *before* the macro window. But the macro itself can add a surge of volatility that triggers a quick move to grab liquidity or spike to your target.

Alternatively, a setup may form *during* the macro window and complete (hit target) within that same window.

The takeaway: Be at your charts 10 minutes before the hour. If you see a setup forming, the macro could be the catalyst that materializes the move.

## Recap

- **ICT Asian Range** runs 07:00 PM — 12:00 AM NY time. Tight range = pending expansion.
- Trade the Asian Range with a directional bias: wait for sweep of high/low, enter on lower timeframe CISD or MSS.
- **NY Session Strategy:** Fibonacci from London low to high → wait for OTE retracement → enter on 1-minute MSS.
- **ICT Macros** happen every hour in the last 10 minutes and first 10 minutes of the hour.
