## Learning Objectives
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to:
- Explain what a Breaker Block is (failed Order Block)
- Recognize a **bullish breaker block** (and its prerequisites)
- Recognize a **bearish breaker block** (and its prerequisites)
- Understand why it often appears after liquidity sweep / market structure shift

## Lesson Script

### 1) Hook: why “failed” order blocks matter (0:00–1:30)
Order Blocks tell you where institutional interest might be.  
But sometimes the market **breaks** the Order Block first.

When that happens, ICT uses a powerful follow-up structure:

> A breaker block is a **failed order block** that shows up after liquidity sweep or market structure shift.

That’s why it’s called the “breaker.”

---

### 2) Definition (1:30–3:00)
From the book:
- “A breaker block is a failed order block which is identified after a liquidity sweep or market structure shift.”
- “When an order block forms in contrast to the prevailing market’s trend, there is high probability of that order block being fail.”

In other words:
- OB appears
- Then market sweeps / shifts structure
- OB fails
- That failed zone can act as new support/resistance

---

### 3) Bullish Breaker Block (3:00–9:30)
The book’s bullish idea:
- Price breaks a **bearish** order block (closing above the high of the bearish OB)
- That broken bearish zone then pushes price higher
- This is the **bullish breaker block**

#### Validation sequence (conditions)
To identify a valid **bullish breaker block**, the book emphasizes:

1) **Liquidity Sweep**
   - You typically expect a sweep event that clears sell-side liquidity.

2) **A valid bearish order block**
   - Don’t guess—your starting OB must be valid by OB rules.

3) **Price closing above the high of the bearish order block**
   - Confirmation that the bearish OB is broken.

4) **Market structure shift to the upside**
   - Structure shifts because delivery is now bullish.

#### Precision note from the book
The book highlights:
- You can consider “consecutive up closed candles,” but for precision
- ICT mostly uses the **last sensitive candle**
- That final candle is the one to anchor your breaker block zone.

**To say on camera:**  
“Bullish breaker block = bearish OB fails, then close above it, with liquidity sweep + upside structure shift.”

---

### 4) Bearish Breaker Block (9:30–14:30)
Opposite logic:
- Price breaks below a **bullish** order block (closing below the low of the bullish OB)
- That broken zone then acts as resistance
- That becomes the bearish breaker block

#### Validation sequence (conditions)
To identify a valid **bearish breaker block**, you check:

1) **Liquidity Sweep**
2) **A valid bullish order block**
3) **Price closing below the low of the bullish order block**
4) **Market structure shift to the downside**

#### Precision note
The book again stresses:
- consecutive down-closed candles can be mentioned conceptually
- but in practice you anchor on the **final down-close candle**
- for the most reactive breaker level.

---

### 5) Quick Practical Workflow (14:30–16:30)
When you see liquidity sweep / shift in structure:
1) Locate the relevant prior **Order Block**
2) Check if price **breaks and closes** beyond OB boundaries
3) Confirm structure shift direction
4) Mark the breaker zone using the **last sensitive candle** approach

---

## Practice / Assignment
- On a replay chart:
  1) Find one bearish OB
  2) Wait for a liquidity sweep + upside close beyond it
  3) Mark the bullish breaker zone
- Repeat for a bearish breaker:
  1) Find one bullish OB
  2) Wait for sweep + downside close below it
  3) Mark the bearish breaker zone
- For each, write the 4 conditions in checklist form.

## Recap (Last 30 seconds)
- Breaker Block = failed OB after sweep or structure shift.
- Bullish breaker: bearish OB broken + close above + upside MSS.
- Bearish breaker: bullish OB broken + close below + downside MSS.
- Precision: last sensitive candle matters.
